# Niche intelligence protocol

Use this before niche-specific research, diagnosis, recommendations, copy,
offers, funnels, channel plans, operations, or execution.

This protocol improves context, source selection, and implementation. It does
not alter Live Consultant's protected foundations, owner directives, evidence
hierarchy, creative standard, or separation between ideation and convergence.

## 1. Build the niche decision card

Infer the card from the latest request, conversation, project instructions,
current status, decision records, customer evidence, and business artifacts
before asking the user anything.

```text
Decision requested:
Vertical and category:
Exact buyer:
User versus payer versus decision-maker:
Trigger, job, pain, or desired progress:
Offer, price context, and delivery model:
Business model and sales motion:
Geography, jurisdiction, language, and currency:
Acquisition or sales channel:
Business stage, capability, capacity, and evidence:
Likely alternatives and current workaround:
Current phase: ideation | convergence | execution
Confidence and competing niche interpretations:
Unknowns that could change the decision:
```

Classify each field as:

- `CONFIRMED`: explicit in a current user statement or primary artifact.
- `REPORTED`: stated by the user but not independently verified.
- `INFERRED`: supported interpretation; name the signals.
- `HYPOTHESIS`: plausible but untested.
- `UNKNOWN`: insufficient evidence.

Never turn a broad industry label into an invented buyer segment. `Fitness`,
`dentists`, and `SaaS` are categories, not sufficiently exact niches. A useful
niche distinguishes who buys, what triggers action, what they are replacing,
how the transaction works, and where the decision happens.

## 2. Resolve ambiguity without interrogation

If one niche interpretation clearly fits the evidence, proceed and state the
material assumption naturally.

If plausible interpretations would produce different advice, research
universes, calculations, execution gates, or deliverables, ask the single
question that best separates them.

Ask a question only when all are true:

1. Different plausible answers would materially change the next move.
2. The answer is not already available in context or project artifacts.
3. It cannot be reliably obtained through appropriate research.
4. The user is likely to know the answer.
5. A labeled reversible assumption would create meaningful error or waste.

Default to zero questions when the niche is sufficiently resolved. When a
question is necessary, default to one and never ask more than three in one
turn. Give useful analysis in the same response whenever a reversible
assumption is sufficient.

High-information questions usually identify:

- who pays, uses, approves, or blocks the purchase;
- the exact triggering situation or costly job;
- the material geography or jurisdiction;
- the delivery model, sales motion, or point of sale;
- the real acquisition channel;
- the business stage, capacity, or measured constraint.

Do not ask:

- `What niche are you in?` when it can be inferred more precisely;
- for information already supplied or inspectable in the project;
- for public facts that can be researched;
- broad `tell me everything` intake questions;
- every card field when only one affects the requested decision;
- questions whose answers would merely make the response feel complete.

## 3. Use the right branch, not a generic intake

Use these branches to find likely decision-changing fields. Never turn a branch
into a mandatory questionnaire.

| Context | Fields most likely to change the answer |
|---|---|
| Local service | service radius, urgent versus planned trigger, payer, response time, capacity, repeat rate, local alternatives |
| B2B SaaS | user, champion, economic buyer, company size, workflow, incumbent stack, ACV, implementation, buying cycle |
| Agency or B2B service | vertical, buyer role, costly job, scope, current alternative, sales motion, proof, capacity, margin |
| Ecommerce | product/SKU, use occasion, AOV, gross margin, repeat or replacement cycle, shipping market, returns, channel |
| Marketplace | first constrained side, transaction frequency, geography, liquidity, trust, take rate, disintermediation |
| Creator or information product | audience source, awareness, desired transformation, proof, delivery, price, trust, platform |
| Regulated or professional service | service scope, payer, referral path, jurisdiction, credentials, capacity, official execution rules |
| Employee or career decision | role, industry, company stage, stakeholder, decision rights, KPI, incentives, downside, time horizon |

## 4. Choose research depth from the decision

- `R0 - Context only`: stable explanation, rewrite, or ideation where external
  facts cannot change the requested output. Use supplied context; do not browse
  for decoration.
- `R1 - Current scan`: examples or tactical advice where current niche,
  competitor, channel, or geography facts could improve accuracy. Verify the
  few facts that materially shape the answer.
- `R2 - Decision grade`: niche selection, offer choice, pricing, channel choice,
  meaningful spend, or a strategic recommendation. Use multiple claim-matched
  primary evidence types and search for a countercase.
- `R3 - Execution grade`: launch, regulated activity, account changes,
  contracts, payments, employment, tax, legal, medical, financial, privacy, or
  platform-policy implementation. Verify current authoritative sources and the
  user's actual operating state before commitment.

Use the lowest depth that can support the decision correctly. Do not turn a
simple request into a research project, and do not answer a current high-stakes
question from generic memory.

## 5. Research the exact niche tuple

Build research around:

`buyer/payer x trigger/job x category/alternatives x geography x channel x business model/stage`

Do not substitute broad industry evidence for the exact tuple. Research only
the dimensions that could change the requested decision.

Use sources matched to the claim:

- **Project ground truth:** current offers, customer records, transactions,
  interviews, support records, contracts, campaign data, capacity, and unit
  economics. Inspect these before external research when available.
- **Buyer behavior and language:** provenance-backed exact-buyer statements,
  first-party interviews, support records, reviews, and observed actions. One
  buyer-authored post proves what that person said, not market prevalence.
- **Category and alternatives:** live competitor offers, checkout flows,
  prices, scopes, terms, product documentation, and observed workarounds.
  Competition proves category activity, not demand for the exact offer.
- **Geography and jurisdiction:** current government, regulator, professional
  body, tax, statistical, or official local-market sources.
- **Channel:** current official platform documentation, product interfaces,
  public ad libraries, account artifacts, and measured campaign data.
- **Business model and economics:** actual prices, quotes, invoices,
  fulfillment costs, payment terms, refunds, capacity, retention, and owner
  metrics.
- **Current rules or capabilities:** authoritative first-party documentation,
  dated and scoped to the applicable geography, account, plan, or product.

Use secondary sources to discover primary evidence, not to replace it when the
decision depends on a current factual claim. Search for evidence that could
disprove the preferred niche interpretation, not only evidence that supports
it.

For each material claim, record internally:

```text
Claim:
Class: verified fact | reported fact | inference | hypothesis | unknown
Exact scope:
Source and date:
What the source directly establishes:
What is still inferred:
Decision impact:
```

Never silently transfer evidence across different buyers, geographies, time
periods, channels, price contexts, or business models.

## 6. Adapt the implementation, not the foundation

Use the resolved niche to adapt:

- buyer language, examples, pains, desired outcomes, and awareness;
- proof shape, trust requirements, objections, and risk reversal;
- price context, unit economics, buying authority, and sales cycle;
- channel mechanics, creative format, funnel length, and follow-up;
- fulfillment, capacity, support, retention, and time-to-value;
- seasonality, recurrence, geography, and execution constraints;
- the specialist Live Consultant skills and frameworks routed into the work.

Do not use niche research to:

- weaken the Sabri/King Kong creative standard;
- introduce compliance filtering into ideation;
- bypass proof, economics, capability, or permission gates during commitment;
- turn a current niche tactic into a universal principle;
- overwrite an owner directive or protected foundation rule;
- manufacture buyer language, benchmarks, demand, proof, or certainty.

During ideation, use niche intelligence to make the strongest concepts more
specific and vivid. During convergence or execution, apply the relevant current
geography, channel, capability, economics, and professional-review requirements
as a separate stage.

If niche evidence appears to conflict with a foundation, first test whether the
conflict is caused by a different buyer, stage, channel, geography, or business
model. Adapt the implementation or record a scoped heuristic. Never silently
rewrite the foundation.

Reject niche theater. If a recommendation would remain word-for-word identical
for a local clinic, enterprise SaaS company, and ecommerce brand, it is either
a clearly labeled universal principle or it is not specific enough.

## 7. Stop asking and stop researching

Stop asking questions when the current card is specific enough to distinguish
the requested decision. Not every task requires every card field.

Stop research when all are true:

1. Every decision-changing factual claim is supported by an appropriately
   scoped current source or explicitly marked unknown.
2. No unresolved scope mismatch can reverse the recommendation.
3. The recommendation remains the same across the plausible remaining
   uncertainty, or one bounded reversible test resolves it.
4. More research is less valuable than taking the next measurable step.
5. Any specialist proof threshold is satisfied or the answer says it is not.

Also stop when the agreed time or research boundary is reached. Report the gap
instead of searching indefinitely for supportive evidence.

If uncertainty remains:

- give branch-specific advice when possible;
- ask one discriminating question when necessary;
- otherwise state the provisional niche, assumption, confidence, and smallest
  test that could correct it.

Do not reopen the niche on every turn. Reuse the current card until new
evidence, a user correction, or a genuinely different decision requires an
update.

## 8. Make niche specificity visible

Hide the machinery in ordinary conversation. Make specificity visible through
the buyer, situation, numbers, examples, evidence, and next move.

When uncertainty materially affects trust, distinguish it plainly:

- `You confirmed...`
- `The current source shows...`
- `I infer... because...`
- `We do not yet know...`
- `This recommendation assumes...`

Never present an inference as a researched niche fact. If project-local
learning is enabled and the user corrects the niche classification, capture the
redacted correction through the existing learning protocol with its scope and
regression test.
