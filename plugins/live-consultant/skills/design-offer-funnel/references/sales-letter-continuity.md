# Sales-letter continuity protocol

Use this protocol for long-form landing pages, homepages, sales letters,
advertorials, webinar pages, and education-led booking pages. It converts the
complete internal marketing analysis into one buyer-facing argument without
making the reader study the framework.

## Governing distinction

The consultant needs the full model. The buyer needs the next clear idea.

Keep research tables, awareness labels, funnel maps, the claim ledger, the
objection map, and the offer-construction framework backstage. Surface their
buyer-facing substance: the buyer's situation, the useful explanation, the
consequence, direct objections and answers, the offer and its terms, the proof,
and any scope or truth-status qualifier that materially changes how the proof
should be understood. Completeness belongs in the reasoning. Effortless
comprehension belongs on the page.

Choose among long copy, short copy, visual demonstration, technical language,
and other page forms by buyer awareness, sophistication, price, risk, intent,
product type, and sales motion. When a long-form argument is the chosen path,
make it continuous.

## 1. Build the belief spine before the page

List the smallest set of beliefs the reader must accept in order. A common
education-led service sequence is:

```text
recognize the situation
-> understand why the usual explanation is incomplete
-> see the cost of leaving it unchanged
-> believe a different mechanism can work
-> see specific proof
-> understand what the business does
-> resolve the largest remaining objections
-> want the offer
-> take the next action
```

Adapt the sequence rather than copying it mechanically. Hot traffic may need a
short proof-to-offer path. Complex B2B may require stakeholder and technical
beliefs. A product demonstration may carry more belief than several paragraphs.

Give every paragraph and component one belief job. If two sections can swap
places without weakening the argument, test whether they are disconnected
modules rather than a causal sales letter.

Keep one shared belief spine until the buyer, offer, or decision path
materially diverges. Then branch deliberately and preserve continuity inside
each branch. A purposeful Agency-or-Courses choice or a multi-stakeholder B2B
path is different from a page that fragments before the shared case is made.

## 2. Write from square zero

Assume the reader is intelligent but does not know the consultant's vocabulary.
Start with events the reader could watch happen:

- a phone that rings one month and goes quiet the next;
- a person who fills out a form and receives no reply;
- a sales team that calls once and stops;
- an invoice that arrives without a clear result;
- a buyer who visits, hesitates, compares, and leaves.

Prefer literal actions such as `calls`, `messages`, `books`, `fills out the
form`, `answers`, `shows up`, and `buys` over shorthand such as `raises a hand`,
`enters the journey`, `moves down the funnel`, or `converts`. Shorthand remains
available when the intended audience already uses it or when it is immediately
translated into an observable action.

Run the novice paraphrase test: after one read, can the intended buyer explain
the point in their own words without borrowing the consultant's labels?

## 3. Create causal momentum

Write the full plain explanation before polishing headlines. Then derive:

1. the shortest headline that states the new belief;
2. one pull line that makes the consequence or payoff vivid; and
3. one transition that creates the next question.

Use one important idea per paragraph. Vary sentence length for rhythm, but do
not hide a simple point inside an elegant sentence. A strong transition makes
the next section feel necessary, not merely available below.

Humor may release tension, expose an absurdity, or make a truth memorable. It
must sharpen a point the reader already understands. If the joke carries the
explanation or interrupts trust, rewrite or remove it.

## 4. Translate proof into business meaning

Do not stop at the platform metric. Translate proof through three layers:

```text
raw change -> business result protected or created -> buyer meaning
```

Illustrative pattern:

```text
lead cost fell from A to B
-> the same gross profit remained
-> the business kept the result and removed the wasted bill
```

Use the strongest accurate contrast as the proof headline. Keep the underlying
numbers close enough that the reader can verify the conclusion without hunting.
Place proof at the moment the reader would naturally doubt the preceding claim.
Concentrated proof can answer broad skepticism after the core mechanism is
understood; a single decisive proof point can answer a specific doubt earlier.

## 5. Make visuals pay rent

Apply the visual necessity test to every chart, card, diagram, timeline,
illustration, photograph, and animation:

1. What exact sentence before it does the visual prove or clarify?
2. What exact sentence after it does the visual prepare?
3. What factual or demonstrative payload disappears if the visual is removed?
4. Does the visual reduce interpretation, or make the reader learn another
   interface?

Keep the visual when it demonstrates the product, makes a comparison visible,
compresses technical complexity, supplies credible proof, or helps the buyer
choose. When its only job is variety, decoration, motion, or exposing the
consultant's framework, compare it against a prose-only version and select the
version that creates faster comprehension with less interpretation.

Copy-synced motion should change the evidence the reader is currently
considering. A bar can contract while a protected outcome stays fixed. A
product can visibly complete the promised task. Decorative motion that competes
with the sentence fails this test.

## 6. Earn the offer and action

A clear above-the-fold offer and CTA may serve ready buyers when the niche,
proof, and promise warrant it. Treat that as a ready-buyer exit, then return to
the education sequence. Repeated offer blocks do not substitute for belief.
Earn the intensified or restated offer after the reader has enough recognition,
explanation, mechanism, proof, and objection resolution for that action to make
sense.

Later CTAs may reflect the reader's current belief instead of repeating the
same line. A first action can promise discovery; a later action can promise a
specific plan; the close can contrast action with the cost of continued delay.
Keep one primary action clear throughout the journey even when its wording
develops.

## 7. Run the deletion and skim passes

### Framework-removal test

Remove the labels, numbers, boxes, or diagram. If the explanation stops making
sense, rewrite the prose so the idea can stand alone before deciding whether
the framework still improves it.

### Visual-removal test

Temporarily remove each non-text element. If comprehension stays equal or
improves, leave it out. If proof, demonstration, comparison, or choice becomes
harder, restore and simplify it.

### Headline-only test

Read only the headings, proof headlines, and CTAs. They should preserve the
argument's direction without becoming a disconnected list of claims.

### Three-reader test

Review as:

1. a category novice who knows the problem but not the terminology;
2. a skeptical buyer disappointed by previous alternatives; and
3. an impatient mobile reader who will not decode a diagram for the writer.

Reject the draft when any of them must translate jargon, remember a framework,
or cross an unexplained logical gap.

## Countercases and scope

Do not overgeneralize this protocol:

- Ecommerce often needs product photography, comparison, and demonstration.
- Technical B2B buyers may understand an accurate architecture diagram faster
  than several paragraphs.
- Warm, high-intent traffic may need a short purchase or booking path rather
  than an awareness-building letter.
- Expert audiences may prefer precise domain language over beginner teaching.
- Regulated or high-stakes categories may require restrained humor and visible
  qualification.
- Long copy still fails when mobile rhythm, hierarchy, proof, or loading is
  weak.
- A guarantee-first hero can create disbelief when the page lacks relevant
  evidence or the audience needs a different first belief.

These are routing variables, not vetoes. Select the page form that reduces the
buyer's real uncertainty and advances the next decision.

## Regression prompts

Use these prompts when reviewing a future release:

1. **Novice service owner:** Draft an education-led homepage for an owner who
   understands their business but not marketing terminology. The page should
   use observable business events, one belief per section, proof beside doubt,
   and an earned offer.
2. **Warm ecommerce buyer:** Draft a short product page where demonstration and
   comparison deserve more space than education. The protocol must keep useful
   visuals rather than forcing a text-only letter.
3. **Technical B2B committee:** Explain a complex system to expert users and an
   economic buyer. The protocol must allow precise terms and a diagram while
   preserving a clear decision sequence.
4. **Education with a ready-buyer exit:** Open a long education-led page with a
   clear evidence-supported offer and CTA for ready buyers, then continue one
   uninterrupted belief spine before intensifying the offer near the close.
5. **High-stakes professional service:** Build trust with precise proof and
   restrained rhetoric. The protocol must not require humor or a guarantee-led
   opening.

The first regression fails when the copy exposes the marketing framework,
relies on ambiguous shorthand, isolates proof, decorates the page with
non-evidence motion, or repeats the offer before belief is earned. The other
countercases fail when the protocol is misread as a universal ban on visuals,
technical language, short pages, early ready-buyer exits, deliberate route
branches, or alternate opening structures.

## Definition of done

A long-form page passes when:

- the intended reader can explain the problem, mechanism, proof, offer, and
  next action after one read;
- every section advances one necessary belief;
- the copy uses buyer-visible events or immediately translates specialist
  terms;
- proof answers the nearest material doubt;
- every visual carries proof, demonstration, comparison, or decision value;
- an early ready-buyer CTA, when used, does not keep interrupting the education
  sequence, and the intensified offer arrives when the reader has enough reason
  to want it;
- the recommended structure still changes appropriately for the countercases.
