## Example 1: local HVAC replacement

### Niche context

- Buyer: homeowner with an aging central-air system during a heat wave.
- Awareness: problem aware and urgently comparing contractors.
- Dominant fear: a surprise breakdown, days without cooling, and an inflated
  emergency invoice.
- Sales motion: search ad -> diagnostic page -> same-day booking -> in-home
  consultation.

### Dream Buyer research hypothesis

Likely language to verify:

- “AC running but house still hot”;
- “repair or replace?”;
- “how much will this really cost?”;
- “I can't wait a week for somebody to show up”;
- fear of being sold a system before the existing one is properly tested.

### Strong first-pass offer

> The Same-Day Cool-Home Decision: we diagnose the system, show repair versus
> replacement costs side by side, and if a replacement is the right move, we
> reserve the earliest installation slot before we leave. Illustrative bonus:
> a temporary cooling plan if the home cannot safely wait.

### HVCO

`The 7 Warning Signs Your AC Is About to Fail—and the One Test That Tells You
Whether Repairing It Is Throwing Good Money After Bad`

Content:

1. observable warning signs;
2. repair-versus-replacement decision tree;
3. questions to ask a contractor;
4. example total-cost comparison;
5. emergency-preparation checklist;
6. CTA to book the diagnostic.

### Opt-in bullets

- The temperature pattern that signals an airflow problem rather than a dead
  unit.
- The repair quote that often means replacement deserves a real comparison.
- What to photograph before the technician arrives so the diagnosis starts
  faster.

### Pressure choices

- **Search/high intent:** lead with same-day decision speed and the cost of an
  emergency breakdown.
- **Cold social:** dramatize the first heat-wave failure and offer the warning
  sign guide.
- **Retargeting:** use proof, diagnostic transparency, and the cost comparison.

### Magic Lantern milestones

1. Identify failure risk.
2. Understand repair versus replacement.
3. Calculate comfort and operating-cost tradeoffs.
4. Prepare the home and questions.
5. Book the diagnosis.

### Measurement

- search click-to-booking rate;
- HVCO opt-in rate;
- diagnostic show rate;
- qualified replacement opportunity rate;
- booked job contribution after install and callbacks.

## Example 2: B2B cybersecurity assessment

### Niche context

- Buyer: COO or IT lead at a 50–250 employee professional-services firm.
- Trigger: client security questionnaire, insurance renewal, or recent vendor
  incident.
- Fear: discovering a serious gap during a client review or after an incident.
- Sales motion: outbound/content -> assessment -> multi-stakeholder proposal.

### Halo research targets

Inspect current cyber-insurance questionnaires, procurement requirements,
industry forums, security audit objections, competitor reviews, and sales-call
notes. Separate the economic buyer's language from the technical user's.

### Strong first-pass offer

> The 10-Day Client-Trust Exposure Map: identify the security gaps most likely
> to cost a deal or delay insurance renewal, rank the first five fixes, and give
> leadership a plain-English action map. Illustrative risk reversal: if the map
> does not identify at least three prioritized, evidence-backed actions, the
> assessment fee is credited or refunded.

### HVCO

`The Client Security Questionnaire Decoder: 18 Answers That Trigger Follow-Up
Questions—and What to Have Ready Before Procurement Asks`

### Sales-message pressure

Use consequences without being vague:

- delayed deal review;
- executive time consumed by unprepared answers;
- insurer exclusions or increased scrutiny;
- loss of buyer confidence.

The message can shine a floodlight on these stakes. The call should then switch
to doctor-style diagnosis because the purchase is complex, multi-stakeholder,
and high trust.

### Doctor-style call

1. What triggered the assessment now?
2. Which customer, insurer, or board requirement is creating pressure?
3. Where is evidence scattered today?
4. What happened during the last questionnaire or audit?
5. What is the commercial and personal consequence if the next review exposes
   the same gap?
6. On a one-to-ten scale, how important is it to resolve this before the next
   customer or insurance deadline—and why?
7. Is the current patchwork actually producing the evidence leadership needs,
   or is it time to replace it with one owned system?
8. What must leadership be able to say or show in 90 days?
9. Which systems, stakeholders, and buying approvals are in scope?
10. Deliver one live gap observation and a short first-action map.
11. If fit exists, ask whether this is the kind of help they want, then
    prescribe the assessment and implementation sequence in their language.

The importance rating, consequence question, and summary in step 7 are the
Admission mechanism. They deliberately invite the buyer to state that the
current approach is inadequate and that change matters.

### Decisive-close variants

For a single authorized buyer who can purchase the assessment now, test the
source-style same-call close:

- surface every non-price concern;
- ask the buyer why they believe the engagement is a fit;
- state the investment and explain it without apologizing;
- offer an explicitly modeled setup-fee waiver for completing onboarding on the
  call;
- ask to proceed, hold silence, and move directly to payment and kickoff after
  agreement.

For a real buying committee, keep the same pressure logic but change the
advance: secure the economic buyer, technical reviewer, decision meeting,
required evidence, and dated next step. Do not pretend a committee can make a
same-call payment decision when the authority structure says otherwise.

### Follow-up command examples

- `Open the five-gap exposure map and mark the first missing artifact now.`
- `Choose the assessment window before the insurer questionnaire lands.`
- `Send the stakeholder list here so the evidence plan can be built.`

These are original examples of the source's direct-command and embedded-command
concept. Test them against softer invitations and measure qualified next-step
completion, not clicks alone.

### Companion skills

- `founder-playbook-spin-selling` for implication and multi-call advances;
- `founder-playbook-influence` for authority and stakeholder proof;
- `founder-playbook-monetizing-innovation` for package and willingness to pay;
- `build-business-operations` for delivery ownership.

## Example 3: DTC dog-shedding kit

### Niche context

- Buyer: dog owner who loves the pet but is embarrassed by hair on clothes,
  furniture, and car seats.
- Awareness: problem aware; has likely tried lint rollers or a vacuum attachment.
- Sales motion: cold Meta ad -> product page or low-friction entry offer ->
  checkout -> repeat consumables / complementary product.

### Buyer language hypothesis

Validate phrases from low-star reviews, pet communities, search, and comments:

- hair reappears immediately after cleaning;
- tools work on one surface but fail on another;
- rollers create constant refill cost;
- guests, dark clothing, and the car create embarrassment.

### Godfather entry offer

> Illustrative “Whole-Home Hair Reset Kit”: reusable tools for couch, carpet,
> clothes, and car, plus a five-minute room sequence and quick-start video.
> First-pass risk reversal: test it on the buyer's three worst surfaces; if it
> does not visibly lift embedded hair, use the stated return remedy.

### Ad angles by temperature

- **Cold / pattern interrupt:** a black-shirt or car-seat demonstration with a
  one-sentence hair problem callout.
- **Problem aware:** “Why the hair comes back after vacuuming” mechanism angle.
- **Hot / retargeting:** surface demonstration, bundle stack, review proof, and
  risk reversal.

### 17-step compression for a product page

1. Dog-owner callout.
2. Visible result headline.
3. One-line mechanism.
4. Three curiosity bullets.
5. Vivid frustration across couch, clothes, and car.
6. Show the multi-surface solution.
7. Demonstrate use and durability.
8. Translate each tool into a surface/result benefit.
9. Add real reviews and demonstrations when available.
10. Present the kit.
11. Add a relevant travel or storage bonus.
12. Stack components.
13. Reveal price.
14. Use an actual inventory, shipping, or launch reason if urgency is selected.
15. State the return or performance remedy.
16. One add-to-cart CTA.
17. P.S. restating the mess avoided and next action.

### Economics to model

- landed product and fulfillment cost;
- payment and return cost;
- bundle contribution;
- cost per checkout and purchase;
- refund and defect rate;
- repeat or upsell contribution;
- working capital and delivery time.

## Example 4: executive recruiting service

### Niche context

- Buyer: founder of a post-seed B2B SaaS company hiring the first VP Sales.
- Pain: months lost, wrong candidates, investor pressure, and a costly mis-hire.
- Sales motion: referral/outbound -> diagnostic session -> retained search.

### Larger Market content path

- **Buying now:** “VP Sales search kickoff” with a direct strategy call.
- **Information gathering:** scorecard and interview-plan HVCO.
- **Problem aware:** content showing why a top individual contributor is not
  automatically a first sales leader.
- **Not yet aware:** stories and benchmarks about the operating cost of an
  unclear role before search begins.

### Offer concept

> The First-VP-Sales Hiring Command Center: role scorecard, market map,
> calibrated interview system, reference process, and 90-day success handoff.
> Illustrative guarantee direction: if an agreed search milestone is missed,
> continue the defined work or apply a fee remedy.

### Magic Lantern sequence

1. Score the role before finding people.
2. Separate builder, scaler, and enterprise profiles.
3. Build the interview evidence matrix.
4. Run references against the real job.
5. Design the first 90 days.

Each step produces a useful artifact. The retained search becomes the fastest
way to execute the system rather than a mysterious candidate database.

## Example 5: accounting SaaS for construction subcontractors

### Niche context

- User: office manager or owner handling job costs and late invoices.
- Trigger: margin surprise, messy month-end close, or cash gap.
- Alternative: spreadsheets plus generic accounting software.
- Sales motion: search/content -> template or calculator -> product demo ->
  subscription.

### HVCO and bridge

`The Job-Profit Leak Calculator: Find Which Active Jobs Are Quietly Eating This
Month's Cash Before Payroll Does`

After the calculation, show:

1. where the leak came from;
2. the smallest repair;
3. how automatic job-cost capture prevents recurrence;
4. a demo using the prospect's workflow.

### Sell-the-click ad

The ad should sell the discovery of the hidden job leak, not attempt to explain
the entire SaaS product. The calculator sells the result; the demo sells the
workflow; the trial sells habitual use.

### Integration

- `founder-playbook-obviously-awesome` clarifies spreadsheets and generic
  accounting tools as the competitive alternatives;
- `founder-playbook-storybrand` simplifies homepage hierarchy;
- `founder-playbook-monetizing-innovation` tests price packages;
- `founder-playbook-lean-startup` defines activation and retention experiments.

## Example quality test

An example fails if it could be pasted into five unrelated industries without
changing the mechanism. It passes when the buyer, trigger, problem, proof,
delivery, channel, and next step all reflect the actual niche.
