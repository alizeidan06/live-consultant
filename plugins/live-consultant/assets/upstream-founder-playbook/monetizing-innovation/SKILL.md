# Monetizing Innovation

> "Design the product around the price." - Ramanujam & Tacke

## Core Insight

**72% of new products fail** (Simon-Kucher 2014, n=1,615). Root cause: pricing is decided LAST instead of designing the product around it. This figure comes from Simon-Kucher's own client survey data and has not been independently verified. The authors are principals at Simon-Kucher, a pricing consultancy - the stat motivates hiring firms like theirs. Treat it as directionally correct but not independently validated.

| Old paradigm | New paradigm |
|--------------|--------------|
| design → build → market → price | market & price → design → build |

Only ~5% of business cases include real WTP (willingness-to-pay) data. Most companies wait until weeks before launch to set price.

---

## The 4 Failure Types (Diagnose Before Fixing)

| Failure | Definition | Cultural Cause | Tell-Tale Signs |
|---------|------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Feature Shock** | Cram too many features → confusing, overpriced | Engineering-driven | Can't articulate value; price slashed post-launch |
| **Minivation** | Right product, priced too low | Risk-averse | Sales easily hits target; sellouts; channel maxes margin |
| **Hidden Gem** | Great idea never properly launched | Coddles core business | Mid-level execs kill it; sold as deal sweetener; rival ships first |
| **Undead** | Wrong answer (or no question asked) | Top-down, no dissent | Sales avoid raising it; pet project of senior management |

**Real examples:** Amazon Fire Phone (Feature Shock, $170M write-down), Audi Q7 (Minivation, missed €210M/yr), Kodak digital camera 1974 (Hidden Gem), Segway/Google Glass (Undead).

Full case file: see [cases.md](cases.md).

## The 5 Pricing Myths (Counter These)

1. "Build it and they will come" - hides 95% of failures
2. "Innovation needs isolated artists" - customer input informs, doesn't pollute
3. "High failure rates are normal" - failure is preventable
4. "Customers must experience product to price it" - false, they react to concepts
5. "You can't price what you haven't built" - cost-plus thinking; price is set by VALUE

| # | Rule | Headline |
|---|------|----------|
| 1 | **WTP Talk Early** | 80% of companies skip this. Have it before design freezes. |
| 2 | **Needs-Based Segmentation** | Demographic segmentation is broken. Segment by needs/value/WTP. |

## Critical Frameworks (At-a-Glance)

### Leaders / Fillers / Killers

| Type | Definition | Action |
|------|------------|--------|
| **Leader** | Drives buying, high WTP | Source emphasis: include it; preserve leader-light, separate, optional, premium-only, substitute, delayed, and deliberately omitted variants when simplicity, cost, strategy, or learning warrants them |
| **Filler** | Nice-to-have | Use to fill gaps |
| **Killer** | Blows the deal if forced to pay | Source action: eliminate or sell à la carte; also test optional, bundled, retained, and segment-specific variants |

Killer test: valued by <20% of customers AND not valued at all by >20%. Killers are segment-dependent (heated seats: leader in cold, killer in tropical).

### Good/Better/Best Distribution

| Distribution | Diagnosis |
|--------------|-----------|
| ≤25% Good, ~70% Better+Best, ≥10% Best | Healthy |
| >50% Good | Trip-wire: cut features from Good |
| Best <10% | Premium tier underpowered |

**Source position on fences:** visible, defensible tier differences reduce cannibalization. Also preserve soft fences, usage gates, service-level differences, nearly identical decoy tiers, and a single-offer configuration; compare comprehension, upgrade rate, margin, and perceived fairness.

### The 5 Monetization Models

| Model | When to Use | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Subscription** | Continual usage | Netflix, Adobe |
| **Dynamic Pricing** | Volatile demand or constrained supply | Uber, airlines |
| **Auctions** | Seller's market, constrained inventory | Google AdWords ($35B/yr), eBay |
| **Pay-As-You-Go / Alternative Metric** | Usage tracks value | Michelin (per-mile), GE engines |
| **Freemium** | Near-zero production AND fixed cost | LinkedIn, Dropbox |

**Freemium source warning:** the cited book reports failure for 90% of companies; software conversion is typically <10%, and the cited game cohort lost 75% of users in day 1. Treat these as context evidence, then model free-trial, reverse-trial, ad-supported, community, subsidized, and paid-only variants against marginal cost, conversion, retention, and network effects.

Models are mix-and-matchable (Costco = subscription + per-product; OpenTable = subscription + transaction fee).

### The 6 Behavioral Pricing Tactics

1. **Compromise effect** - the source favors 3 tiers because people avoid extremes; also test 1, 2, 4+, and decoy configurations
2. **Anchoring** - The Economist test: $59 vs $125 vs $125-print-only made bundle pick rate jump 32% → 84%
3. **Price signals quality** - Ariely placebo: $2.50 pill 85% pain relief, $0.10 same pill 61%
4. **Razor / razor blades** - low upfront preferred even if total cost identical
5. **Pennies-a-day** - $9.99/mo converts very differently from $120/yr
6. **Psychological thresholds** - $69.99 works, $71 doesn't (drops acceptance >20%)

**Source caveat and variants:** behavioral pricing is strongest when paired with rational or value-based justification. Pure behavioral, pure cost-plus, parity, auction, and value-based approaches remain available for comparison on conversion, trust, margin, explainability, and repeat purchase.

## Decision Trees

### "Is my product likely to fail?"

```
Can I clearly state the customer benefit (not features)?
├─ NO  → Source diagnostic emphasis: Feature Shock; compare Minivation, Hidden Gem, Undead, positioning, channel, proof, and mixed explanations
└─ YES → Has WTP been validated with real customers?
         ├─ NO  → Source diagnostic emphasis: Undead or Minivation; compare all four failure types and non-pricing explanations
         └─ YES → Did C-suite engage personally?
                  ├─ NO  → Source diagnostic emphasis: Hidden Gem; compare all failure types, delegated-success, and execution explanations
                  └─ YES → Source signal: stronger alignment; continue testing all failure and success hypotheses
```

### "Which monetization model?"

```
Is value tied to usage?
├─ YES → Source emphasis: Alternative Metric; all five models and hybrids remain available
└─ NO  → Demand volatile or supply constrained?
        ├─ YES → Source emphasis: Dynamic Pricing; all five models and hybrids remain available
        └─ NO  → Production cost near zero?
                ├─ YES → Freemium is favored when the free majority remains economically supportable; otherwise compare subsidized, limited-free, trial, and paid variants
                └─ NO  → Source emphasis: Subscription or per-unit; alternative-metric, dynamic, auction, freemium, bundle, and hybrid variants remain available
```

### "Should I cut the price?"

```
Sales below plan?
└─ YES → Identified ROOT CAUSE?
         ├─ NO  → Source emphasis: diagnose before a broad cut; preserve price-cut-first, promotion-first, value-first, reverse, parallel, and bounded test variants
         └─ YES → Pricing-specific?
                  ├─ NO  → Source emphasis: address the diagnosed cause; preserve price-led, offer-led, message-led, channel-led, reverse, parallel, and strategic-loss variants
                  └─ YES → Tried 3 nonprice actions?
                           ├─ NO  → Source emphasis: test advertise/add-value/upgrade; preserve immediate cut, one-action, 4+ action, reverse, parallel, and price-war variants
                           └─ YES → War-game competitor reaction
                                    └─ Worse off after counter? → source default is hold; alternate branches are targeted cut, temporary promotion, bundle change, or deliberate price-war entry with modeled consequences
```

These trees express source-default diagnostic and design emphases, not exclusive diagnoses, model eligibility, or required order. Preserve all four failure explanations, all monetization models, and every price-response tactic in every branch, including early, late, reverse, parallel, mixed, and full-method variants. Choose using customer value evidence, WTP, cost structure, demand volatility, supply, competitive response, strategic objective, speed, reversibility, trust, and contribution-margin consequences.

## The "BECAUSE" Test

The source asks every pricing decision to end with a "because" traceable to customer data. Keep additional bases visible: cost, competitor parity, strategic penetration, regulation, executive judgment, brand signaling, and deliberate experimentation. Classify the evidence strength rather than suppressing the option.

**Bad:** "We priced at $99 to be competitive."
**Good:** "We priced at $99 BECAUSE 60% of segment B told us $100 was the threshold above which they'd reconsider, and our value advantage justifies the high end."

When the team cannot yet cite customer evidence, the strategy is hypothesis-led rather than customer-validated. It can still be generated and tested; mark assumptions, downside, reversibility, and the next evidence-producing action.

## Source-Default Quick Reference Checklist and Complete Variants

The source foregrounds these before product design freezes. Preserve design-first, price-first, research-first, reverse, parallel, staged, late-pricing, and whole-method variants; mark which items are complete, hypothetical, deferred, or deliberately omitted and state the effect on evidence, speed, cost, and reversibility.
- [ ] WTP conversations with real customers held
- [ ] Segmented by needs/value/WTP (not demographics)
- [ ] Leaders, fillers, killers identified
- [ ] G/B/B configuration designed with FENCES
- [ ] Monetization model picked (aligns with value)
- [ ] Pricing strategy documented (max/penetration/skimming)
- [ ] Living business case links Price/Value/Volume/Cost
- [ ] Benefit-not-feature messaging tested
- [ ] Behavioral tactics considered
- [ ] Team prepared to maintain price integrity post-launch

**The test:** Ask anyone "Why this price?" If the answer is "cost-plus" or "competitor benchmark," failure is coming.

## Lower-Leverage Contexts and Complementary Methods

- Pure cost-plus regulated environments (utilities, some defense): emphasize allowable-cost, procurement, and regulatory models while retaining WTP analysis for design and service levels.
- Pure commodities (sugar, copper): combine index pricing with quality, terms, logistics, hedging, and differentiated-service variants.
- Very early stage with no product: run Mom Test, concept pricing, offer-first ideation, or parallel discovery/WTP variants.
- B2C impulse buys under $50: compare the full process with faster behavioral tests and market experiments.
- Geographic markets with low purchasing power: explore lower price, smaller unit, financing, sponsorship, cross-subsidy, or a different segment; model economics before execution.

## Caveat on WTP Research

Stated WTP and revealed WTP differ. Customers predict their own behavior poorly in interviews. Treat WTP findings as a strong prior, not certainty. Validate with paid pilots, pre-orders, live A/B price tests, or money-back guarantees. See [integration.md](integration.md#wtp-research-limitations).
